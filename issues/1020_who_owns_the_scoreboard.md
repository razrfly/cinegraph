# Marketing: 'Who owns the scoreboard?' — the Letterboxd sale and Cinegraph's two-axis case against rating platforms

## Why this issue exists

On **April 26, 2026** it was reported that **60% of Letterboxd is for sale** — the majority stake [Tiny acquired from the founders in October 2023 at a ~$50–60M valuation](https://variety.com/2023/digital/news/letterboxd-acquired-50-million-deal-valuation-1235740185/). In response, a community-led counter-bid sprang up: social entrepreneur Elizabeth Joyce (via her public-benefit corp **Intrinsic**) and producer Ted Hope launched a [60-day Seed & Spark crowdfunding campaign](https://tedhope.substack.com/p/the-letterboxd-crowdfunding-campaign) to fund an offer and run the platform as a **cooperative** — explicitly to keep it from "passing to another private equity firm or a big corporate owner." It had raised ~$27K when the story broke. ([Deadline](https://deadline.com/2026/05/letterboxd-for-sale-1236921783/), [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/27/letterboxd-sale-film-social-media-semafor-versant-ankler/), [BetaKit](https://betakit.com/letterboxds-canadian-owner-reportedly-looking-to-sell/).)

This is a **content/positioning brief** (like #915 and #571 — no code here). It defines a sharper, harder marketing narrative than our other marketing issues, and ends with **homepage copy and UI/UX wireframes** for how we actually surface "why we're different."

## The one-sentence spine

> **Film discovery is broken — because their agenda is not yours.**
>
> Every mainstream film-rating platform has an agenda, and it isn't "help you find a great movie." It's driven by profit, it's rigged by AI and bots, and *you seeing a good film is not in their prospectus.*

Two phrases we should **repeat relentlessly** across the whole site — hero, about page, footer, social, every surface:

- **"Film discovery is broken."** — the problem, stated flat. It's the opening that earns the rest.
- **"Your agenda is not theirs."** / **"You seeing a good film is not in their prospectus."** — the spine. Say it until it sticks.

Everything below ladders up to those lines. They are the headline, the through-line, and the thing we want a stranger to remember 10 seconds after they land.

## The paradox (the sales pitch — lead with this)

This is the **felt problem**, and it's the most universal, relatable way into everything else. Before we explain *who's at fault* (ownership) or *how the signal got poisoned* (bots), we name the contradiction every single viewer already lives:

> **It's never been easier to watch a film. It's never been harder to find a good one.**

In 2026 you carry, in your pocket, access to more movies than any human in history — a century of cinema spread across Netflix, Prime Video, Max, Disney+, Apple TV+, Hulu, Paramount+, Peacock, Mubi, Criterion, and a dozen more. That abundance would have been pure science fiction to a video-store clerk in 2000.

And yet you spend 40 minutes scrolling, give up, and settle for the same sequel everyone else did. **Everyone knows that feeling.** The abundance didn't fix discovery — it broke it.

That juxtaposition *is* the pitch:

- **More access to films than ever → the worst tools in history to choose a good one.**
- Infinite shelves → and no clerk.
- Every movie ever made, one tap away → and the only thing telling you what's "good" is a bot-fillable number owned by the company that wants you to watch *theirs.*

And here's the part that isn't an accident: **the same companies that gave you the access also own — or feed — the broken signal you use to navigate it.** They solved *distribution* (what they sell) and broke *discovery* (what would actually serve you). Abundance without judgment isn't a gift; it's a maze.

> They gave you every movie ever made and no good way to choose one. **Cinegraph is the choosing part.**

This is the consumer-facing front door to the same thing #857 (the Video Clerk) and #909 (Choose Your Algorithm) argue structurally: abundance + a broken, conflicted signal = paralysis, not discovery.
